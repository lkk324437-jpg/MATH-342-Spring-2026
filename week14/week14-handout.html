    Definition 1. Laplace Transform.

    The Laplace transform of \(u(x,t)\) with respect to \(t\) is defined as
    \begin{equation*} \mathscr{L}\left\{u(x,t)\right\}=\int_0^\infty e^{-st}u(x,t)dt=U(x,s), \end{equation*}
    where \(x\) is treated as a parameter.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.

    In Week 1, we used Laplace Transform to reduce the ODE to an algebraic equation. Here, we will use Laplace Transform to reduce a PDE to an ODE. Here are a few useful properties:
    1. \begin{equation} \mathscr{L}\left\{\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}\right\}=sU(x,s)-u(x,0)\tag{1} \end{equation}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \begin{equation} \mathscr{L}\left\{\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2}\right\}=s^2U(x,s)-su(x,0)-u_t(x,0)\tag{2} \end{equation}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \begin{equation} \mathscr{L}\left\{\frac{\partial ^2 u}{\partial x^2}\right\}=\frac{d^2 U}{d x^2}\tag{3} \end{equation}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.

    Solve the boundary value problem using Laplace Transform:
    \begin{align*} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}=\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2}, \amp \amp 0\lt x\lt 1, t\gt 0 \\ u(0,t)=0, u(1,t)=0, \amp \amp t\gt 0 \\ u(x,0)=0, \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}\biggr|_{t=0}=2\sin\pi x+4\sin 3\pi x, \amp \amp 0\lt x\lt 1 \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 5.

    Integral transforms appear in transform pairs. If \(f(x)\) is transformed into \(F(\alpha)\) by an integral transform
    \begin{equation} F(\alpha)=\int_a^b f(x)K(\alpha,x)dx,\tag{4} \end{equation}
    then the function \(f\) can be recovered by another integral transform
    \begin{equation} f(x)=\int_c^d F(\alpha)H(\alpha,x)d\alpha\tag{5} \end{equation}
    called the inverse transform. The functions \(K\) and \(H\) in (4) and (5) are called kernels of their respective transforms. In Laplace transform, \(K(s,t)=e^{-st}\) and \(H(s,t)=\frac{e^{st}}{2\pi i}.\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 6. Fourier Transform Pairs.

      • Fourier Transform:
        \begin{equation} \mathscr{F}\{f(x)\}=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(x)e^{i\alpha x}dx=F(\alpha)\tag{6} \end{equation}
        🔗
        🔗
      • Inverse Fourier Transform
        \begin{equation} \mathscr{F}^{-1}\{F(\alpha)\}=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}F(\alpha)e^{-i\alpha x}d\alpha=f(x)\tag{7} \end{equation}
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
      • Fourier cosine Transform:
        \begin{equation} \mathscr{F}_c\{f(x)\}=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(x)\cos(\alpha x)dx=F(\alpha)\tag{8} \end{equation}
        🔗
        🔗
      • Inverse Fourier cosine Transform
        \begin{equation} \mathscr{F}_c^{-1}\{F(\alpha)\}=\frac{2}{\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}F(\alpha)\cos(\alpha x)d\alpha=f(x)\tag{9} \end{equation}
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
      • Fourier sine Transform:
        \begin{equation} \mathscr{F}_s\{f(x)\}=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(x)\sin(\alpha x)dx=F(\alpha)\tag{10} \end{equation}
        🔗
        🔗
      • Inverse Fourier sine Transform
        \begin{equation} \mathscr{F}_s^{-1}\{F(\alpha)\}=\frac{2}{\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}F(\alpha)\sin(\alpha x)d\alpha=f(x)\tag{11} \end{equation}
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 7. Existence and Properties of Fourier Transform.

    • Sufficient conditions for existence of (6), (8), and (10) are that \(f\) be absolutely integrable on the appropriate interval and that \(f\) and \(f'\) be piecewise continuous on every finite interval.
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(\mathscr{F}\{1\},\) \(\mathscr{F}_c\{1\}\text{,}\) and \(\mathscr{F}_s\{1\}\) do not exist.
      🔗
      🔗
    • All 6 transforms in Definition 6 are linear transforms.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Suppose that \(f\) is continuous and absolute integrable on the interval \((-\infty, \infty)\) and \(f'\) is piecewise continuous on every finite interval. If \(f(x)\rightarrow 0\) as \(x\rightarrow \pm\infty,\) then
      • \begin{equation} \mathscr{F}\{f'(x)\}=-i\alpha F(\alpha)\tag{12} \end{equation}
        🔗
        🔗
      • With added assumptions that \(f'\) is continuous on \((-\infty,\infty)\text{,}\) \(f''\) is piecewise continuous on every finite interval, and \(f'(x)\rightarrow 0\) as \(x\rightarrow\pm\infty\text{,}\) we have
        \begin{equation} \mathscr{F}\{f''(x)\}=\left(-i\alpha\right)^2\mathscr{F}\{f(x)\}=-\alpha^2F(\alpha) \tag{13} \end{equation}
        🔗
        🔗
      • Similarly, we have
        \begin{equation*} \mathscr{F}\{f^{(n)}(x)\}=(-i\alpha)^nF(\alpha), \end{equation*}
        where \(n=0,1,2,\ldots.\)
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    • For Fourier Cosine Transform and Fourier Sine Transform: under the same assumptions mentioned above:
      • \(\displaystyle \mathscr{F}_c\{f'(x)\}=\alpha \mathscr{F}_s\{f(x)\}-f(0)\)
        🔗
        🔗
      • \(\displaystyle \mathscr{F}_s\{f'(x)\}=-\alpha \mathscr{F}_c\{f(x)\}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      • \(\displaystyle \mathscr{F}_c\{f''(x)\}=-\alpha^2 F(\alpha)-f'(0)\)
        🔗
        🔗
      • \(\displaystyle \mathscr{F}_s\{f''(x)\}=-\alpha^2 F(\alpha)+\alpha f'(0)\)
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

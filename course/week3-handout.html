    Definition 38.

    When the vector differential operator,
    \begin{equation*} \nabla=\textbf{i}\frac{\partial}{\partial x}+\textbf{j}\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\textbf{k}\frac{\partial}{\partial z}, \end{equation*}
    is applied to a differentiable function \(w=f(x,y,z),\) we say that the vector
    \begin{equation*} \nabla f(x,y,z)=\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\textbf{i}+\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\textbf{j}+\frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\textbf{k}, \end{equation*}
    is the gradient of the respective function \(f(x,y,z).\)

    Definition 39.

    The directional derivative of \(z=f(x,y)\) in the direction of a unit vector \(\textbf{u}=\cos\theta\textbf{i}+\sin\theta\textbf{j}\) is
    \begin{equation*} D_\textbf{u}f(x,y)=\lim_{h\rightarrow 0}\frac{f(x+h\cos\theta,y+h\sin\theta)-f(x,y)}{h} \end{equation*}
    provided the limit exists.

    Theorem 40.

    If \(z=f(x,y)\) is a differentiable function of \(x\) and \(y\) and \(\textbf{u}=\cos\theta\textbf{i}+\sin\theta\textbf{j},\) then
    \begin{equation*} D_\textbf{u}f(x,y)=\nabla f(x,y)\cdot\textbf{u}. \end{equation*}
    Similarly, for a function \(m=F(x,y,z)\) and \(\textbf{u}=\cos\alpha\textbf{i}+\cos\beta\textbf{j}+\cos\gamma\textbf{k},\) where \(\alpha, \beta,\) and \(\gamma\) are the direction angles of the unit vector \(\textbf{u}\) measured relative to the positive \(x-, y-\) and \(z-\)axes, respectively, it can be shown that
    \begin{equation*} D_\textbf{u}F(x,y,z)=\nabla F(x,y,z)\cdot\textbf{u}. \end{equation*}

    Remark 41.

    The gradient vector \(\nabla f\) points in the direction in which \(f\) increases most rapidly, whereas \(-\nabla f\) points in the direction of the most rapid decrease of \(f.\)

    Remark 44.

    Let \(F(x,y,z)=c\) be the level surface of a differentiable function \(w=F(x,y,z)\) that passes through \(P(x_0, y_0, z_0).\) If the differentiable function \(\textbf{r}(t)=\langle x=f(t), y=g(t), z=h(t)\rangle\) are the parametric equation of a curve \(C\) on the surface for which \(x_0=f(t_0), y_0=g(t_0),z_0=h(t_0).\) The derivative of \(F(f(t),g(t),h(t))=0\) implies that
    \begin{equation*} \nabla F(x_0,y_0,z_0)\cdot\textbf{r}'(t_0)=0, \end{equation*}
    i.e., \(\nabla F\) is normal to the level surface at \(P.\)

    Theorem 45.

    Let \(P(x_0,y_0,z_0)\) be a point on the graph of \(F(x,y,z)=c,\) where \(\nabla F\) is not \(\textbf{0}.\) Then an equation of the tangent plane at \(P\) is
    \begin{equation*} F_x(x_0,y_0,z_0)(x-x_0)+F_y(x_0,y_0,z_0)(y-y_0)+F_z(x_0,y_0,z_0)(z-z_0)=0 \end{equation*}
    Furthermore, the line containing \(P\) that is parallel to \(\nabla F\) is called the normal line to the surface at P, which can be expressed as symmetric equations
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x-x_0}{F_x(x_0,y_0,z_0)}=\frac{y-y_0}{F_y(x_0,y_0,z_0)}=\frac{z-z_0}{F_z(x_0,y_0,z_0)}, \end{equation*}
    or parametric equations
    \begin{equation*} x=F_x(x_0,y_0,z_0)t+x_0, y=F_y(x_0,y_0,z_0)t+y_0, z=F_z(x_0,y_0,z_0)t+z_0. \end{equation*}

    Definition 49.

    The curl of a vector field \(\textbf{F}=P\textbf{i}+Q\textbf{j}+R\textbf{k}\) is the vector field
    \begin{equation*} curl \textbf{ F}=\left(\frac{\partial R}{\partial y}-\frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}\right)\textbf{i} +\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial z}-\frac{\partial R}{\partial x}\right)\textbf{j} +\left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)\textbf{k}. \end{equation*}
    It can be computed from the cross product,
    \begin{equation*} curl \textbf{ F}=\nabla \times \textbf{F}= \left|\begin{array}{ccc}\textbf{i}\amp \textbf{j}\amp \textbf{k}\\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\amp \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\amp\frac{\partial}{\partial z}\\ P\amp Q \amp R \end{array}\right| \end{equation*}

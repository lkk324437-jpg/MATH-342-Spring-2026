    Remark 82. Review of inner product between two vectors.

    The inner product of two \(n\times 1\) vectors \(u\) and \(v\) is defined as
    \begin{equation*} (u,v)=u^tv=\sum_{i=1}^n u_iv_i. \end{equation*}
    A key property is
    \begin{equation*} u^tv=0\iff u \mbox{ and }v \mbox{ are perpendicular to each other.} \end{equation*}
    It means if \(u^tv=0,\) then the projection of \(u\) onto \(v\) is 0 and the projection of \(v\) onto \(u\) is 0. This makes decomposition of vectors cleaner.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 83.

    The inner product of two functions \(f_1\) and \(f_2\) on an interval \([a,b]\) is
    \begin{equation*} (f_1,f_2)=\int_a^b f_1(x)f_2(x)dx. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 84.

    Two function \(f_1\) and \(f_2\) are said to be orthogonal on an interval \([a,b]\) if
    \begin{equation*} (f_1,f_2)=\int_a^bf_1(x)f_2(x)dx=0. \end{equation*}
    A set of real-valued functions \(\{\phi_1(x),\phi_2(x),\phi_3(x),\ldots\}\) is said to be orthogonal on an interval \([a,b]\) if
    \begin{equation*} (\phi_i,\phi_j)=\int_a^b \phi_i(x)\phi_j(x)dx=0, \forall i\neq j. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 87.

    Legendre’s Equation of order \(n\) is defined as
    \begin{equation} (1-x^2)y''-2xy'+n(n+1)y=0.\tag{..1} \end{equation}

    Remark 89. Properties of Legendre Polynomials.

    Let \(P_n(x)\) be a solution to (..1). \(P_n(x),\) Legendre Polynomial, satisfies the followings:
    • \(\displaystyle P_n(-x)=(-1)^nP_n(x)\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(P_n(1)=1\) and \(P_n(-1)=(-1)^n\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(P_n(0)=0,\) \(n\) odd, and \(P'_n(0)=0,\) \(n\) even
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(\displaystyle (k+1)P_{k+1}(x)-(2k+1)xP_k(x)+kP_{k-1}(x)=0\)
      🔗
      🔗

    Definition 91.

    The norm of a function \(\phi(x)\) is
    \begin{equation*} \|\phi(x)\|=\sqrt{(\phi,\phi)}=\sqrt{\int_a^b\phi^2(x)dx}. \end{equation*}
    If \(\left\{\phi_n(x)\right\}\) is an orthogonal set of functions on the interval \([a,b]\) with \(\|\phi_n(x)\|=1,\) for all \(n,\) then \(\left\{\phi_n(x)\right\}\) is said to be an orthonormal set on the interval of \([a,b].\)

    Definition 95.

    The Fourier Series of a function \(f\) defined on \((-p, p)\) is given by
    \begin{align*} f(x) \amp = \frac{a_0}{2}+\sum_{n=1}^\infty\left(a_n\cos\frac{n\pi}{p}x+b_n\sin\frac{n\pi}{p}x\right),\\ a_0 \amp = \frac{1}{p}\int_{-p}^p f(x)dx\\ a_n \amp = \frac{1}{p}\int_{-p}^p f(x)\cos\frac{n\pi}{p}x dx \\ b_n \amp = \frac{1}{p}\int_{-p}^p f(x)\sin\frac{n\pi}{p}x dx \end{align*}

    Theorem 98. Conditions for Convergences.

    Let \(f\) and \(f'\) be piecewise continuous on the interval of \([-p,p];\) that is, let \(f\) and \(f'\) be continuous except at a finite number of points in the interval and have only finite discontinuities at these points. Then for all \(x\) in the interval \((-p,p)\) the Fourier series of \(f\) converges to \(f(x)\) at a point of continuity. At the point of discontinuity, the Fourier series converges to the average
    \begin{equation*} \frac{f(x+)+f(x-)}{2}, \end{equation*}
    where \(f(x+)\) and \(f(x-)\) denote the limit of \(f\) and \(x\) from the right and from the left, respectively.

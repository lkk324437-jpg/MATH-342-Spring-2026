    Definition 1.

    1. \(i\) is the imaginary unit defined by \(i^2=-1.\)

    2. For \(x\) a real number, Euler’s formula
      \begin{equation} e^{ix}=\cos x+i\sin x \mbox{ gives } e^{-ix}=\cos x-i\sin x. \tag{1} \end{equation}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. A complex number is a number \(z=a+ib,\) where \(a\) and \(b\) are real numbers. The number \(\bar{z}=a-ib\) is called the conjugate of \(z.\)

    Remark 2.

    From (1), we can derive
    \begin{equation} \cos x=\frac{e^{ix}+e^{-ix}}{2}\mbox{ and }\sin x=\frac{e^{ix}-e^{-ix}}{2i} \tag{2} \end{equation}
    Then using (2) to replace \(\cos\frac{n\pi x}{p}\) and \(\sin \frac{n\pi x}{p}\) in the defintion of Fourier Series.

    Definition 3. Complex Fourier Series.

    The complex Fourier series of function \(f\) defined on an interval \((-p,p)\) is given by
    \begin{align} f(x)\amp =\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}c_n e^{in\pi x/p} \tag{3}\\ \mbox{where }c_n \amp = \frac{1}{2p}\int_{-p}^p f(x)e^{-in\pi x/p}dx, n=0,\pm 1,\pm 2,\ldots\notag \end{align}
    If \(f(x)\) is a periodic function and the fundamental period of \(f\) is \(T=2p,\) then (3) becomes \(\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}c_ne^{in\omega x},\) where \(\omega=\frac{2\pi}{T}\) is called fundamental angular frequency and \(c_n\) is called frequency spectrum of \(f.\)
    🔗

    Definition 5. Linear Partial Differential Equation.

    Let \(u\) denote the dependent variable and \(x\) and \(y\) independent variables, the general form of a linear second-order partial differential equations is given by
    \begin{equation} A\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}+B\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x\partial y}+C\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}+D\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+E\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}+Fu=G,\tag{4} \end{equation}
    where the coefficients \(A, B, \ldots, G\) are constants or functions of \(x\) and \(y.\) When \(G(x,y)=0\text{,}\) (4) is said to be homogeneous; otherwise, it is nonhomogeneous.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 6. Separation of Variables.

    While seeking a particular solution of a differential equation in which the independent variable are \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) we assume that this particular solution is in the form of product of a function of \(x\) and a function of \(y\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} u(x,y)=X(x)Y(y). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 8. Superposition Principle.

    If \(u_1, u_2, \ldots, u_k\) are solutions of a homogeneous linear PDE, then the linear combination
    \begin{equation*} u=c_1u_1+c_2u_2+\cdots+c_ku_k, \end{equation*}
    is also a solution, where \(c_i, i=1,2,\ldots, k, \) are constants.

    Definition 10. Classification of linear 2nd-order PDEs.

    The linear 2nd order PDE:
    \begin{equation*} A\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}+B\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x\partial y}+C\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2} +D\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+E\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}+Fu=G, \end{equation*}
    is said to be
    • hyperbolic if \(B^2-4AC\gt 0,\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • parabolic if \(B^2-4AC=0,\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • elliptic if \(B^2-4AC\lt 0.\)
      🔗
      🔗

    Definition 13. 1-D Heat Equation.

    Consider a thin rod of length \(L\) with an initial temperature \(f(x)\) throughout and whose ends are held at temperature zero for all time \(t\gt 0.\) The temperature \(u(x,t)\) in the rod is determined from the BVP:
    \begin{align} k\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}\amp= \frac{\partial u}{\partial t},0\lt x\lt L, t\gt 0 \tag{8}\\ u(0,t)\amp= 0, t\gt 0 \notag\\ u(L,t)\amp= 0, t\gt 0 \notag\\ u(x,0)\amp= f(x),0\lt x\lt L \notag \end{align}
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 15. Wave Equation.

    Consider a string of length \(L,\) stretched taut between two end points on the x-axis. When the string starts to vibrate, assume that the motion takes place in the xy-plane in such a manner that each point on the string moves in a direction perpendicular to the x-axis (transverse vibrations). Let \(u(x,t)\) denote the vertical displacement of any point on the string measured from the x-axis for \(t\gt 0.\) (Referred back to Section 13.2 for further assumptions.)
    \begin{align} a^2\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}\amp =\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2},0\lt x\lt L, t\gt 0 \tag{9}\\ u(0,t)\amp =0, u(L,t)=0, t\gt 0 \notag\\ u(x,0)\amp =f(x),\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}\bigg|_{t=0}=g(x), 0\lt x\lt L \notag \end{align}
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 16. Laplace’s Equation.

    \(u(x,t)\) is the steady-state temperature in a rectangular plate. When no heat escapes from the lateral faces of the plate, we solve the following BVP:
    \begin{align} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}+\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}\amp =0,0\lt x\lt a, 0\lt y\lt b \tag{10}\\ \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\bigg|_{x=0}\amp =0, 0\lt y\lt b \notag\\ \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\bigg|_{x=a}\amp =0, 0\lt y\lt b\notag\\ u(x,0) \amp =0, 0\lt x\lt a \notag\\ u(x,b) \amp =f(x),0\lt x\lt a \notag \end{align}
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 18. Laplace’s Equation-Superposition Principle.

    \begin{align*} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}+\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2} =0,\amp \amp 0\lt x\lt a, 0\lt y\lt b \\ u(0,y)=F(y),\amp \amp u(a,y)=G(y), 0\lt y\lt b \\ u(x,0)=f(x),\amp \amp u(x,b)=f(x), 0\lt x\lt a \end{align*}

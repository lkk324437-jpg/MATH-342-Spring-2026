    Definition 1.

    A power series in \(x-a\) is an infinite series of the form
    \begin{equation} \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-a)^n=c_0+c_1(x-a)+c_2(x-a)^2+\cdots.\tag{0} \end{equation}
    It is also called a power series centered at \(a\text{.}\)

    Definition 8.

    The standard form of a linear second order differential equation is defined as
    \begin{equation*} y''+P(x)y'+Q(x)y=0. \end{equation*}
    A point \(x_0\) is said to be an ordinary point of the differential equation if both \(P(x)\) and \(Q(x)\) in the standard form are analytic at \(x_0.\) A point that is not an ordinary point is said to be a singular point of the equation.

    Example 9.

    Identify all ordinary points and singular points of
    \begin{equation*} (x^2-4x-5)y''+2xy'+3y=0. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Convert to the standard form:
    \begin{equation*} y''+\frac{2x}{x^2-4x-5}y'+\frac{3}{x^2-4x-5}y=0 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \Rightarrow P(x)=\frac{2x}{x^2-4x-5} \mbox{ and } Q(x)=\frac{3}{x^2-4x-5} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x^2-4x-5=0\Rightarrow (x-5)(x+1)=0\Rightarrow x=5 \mbox{ or } x=-1 \end{equation*}
    \(P(x)\) and \(Q(x)\) are analytic at all real numbers, except \(5\) or \(-1.\) Thus singular points for the given DE are \(\{-1,5\}\) and the ordinary points are \(x\neq -1\) and \(x\neq 5,\) i.e., \((-\infty, -1)\bigcup(-1,5)\bigcup(5,\infty).\)

    Theorem 10.

    If \(x=x_0\) is an ordinary point of a 2nd order linear differential equation, we can always find 2 linearly independent solutions in the form of a power series centered at \(x_0\text{;}\) that is \(y=\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-x_0)^n,\) called a solution about the ordinary point \(x_0.\) A series solution converges at least on some interval defined by \(|x-x_0|\lt R,\) where \(R\) is the distance from \(x_0\) to be the closest singular point.

    Theorem 14. Method of Frobenius.

    If \(x=x_0\) is a regular singular point of the 2nd order linear differential equation, then there exists at least one nonzero solution of the form
    \begin{equation*} y=(x-x_0)^r\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-x_0)^n=\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-x_0)^{n+r}, \end{equation*}
    where the number \(r\) is a constant to be determined. The series will converge at least on some interval defined by \(0\lt x-x_0\lt R.\)

    Definition 16.

    After substituting \(y=\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_nx^{n+r}\) into the given differential equation and simplifying, the indicial equation is a equation in \(r\) that results from equating the total coefficient of the lowest power of \(x\) to zero. The solutions to the indicial equation are called indicial roots, or exponents of the singularity \(x=0.\) When the given differential equations are in the standard form of the 2nd order DE, the indicial equation is quadratic.

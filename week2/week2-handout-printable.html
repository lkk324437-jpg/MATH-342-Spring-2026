    Definition 1.

    There are two types of quantities: scalars and vectors.
    • A scalar is a real number or a quantity that has magnitude.

    Definition 2.

    There are several sets of notations to describe a vector or properties related to a vector:
    • \(\overrightarrow{AB}\text{:}\) A vector whose initial point is \(A\) and whose terminal point is \(B.\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(\textbf{u}=\langle u_1,u_2,\ldots,u_n \rangle\) is a vector in \(R^n,\) where \(u_i\) are scalars and said to be the components of the vector \(\textbf{u}\text{.}\) In mathematics, it is often written as a column vector, i.e., \(\left[\begin{array}{c} u_1\cr u_2\cr\vdots\cr u_n\end{array}\right].\) In 2-D, \(\textbf{u}\) can also be written as \(u_1\textbf{i}+u_2\textbf{j}.\) In 3-D, \(\textbf{u}\) can also be written as \(u_1\textbf{i}+u_2\textbf{j}+u_3\textbf{k}.\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • The magnitude, length, or norm of a vector u is denoted by \(\|u\|\text{.}\) A common norm defined in \(R^n\) is
      \begin{equation*} \|\overrightarrow{u}\|=\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n |u_i|^2}. \end{equation*}
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • A vector that has a magnitude of 1 is called a unit vector.

    Definition 6.

    A 3D vector function \(\textbf{r}\) has components that are functions of a parameter \(t\text{,}\) i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} \textbf{r}(t)=\langle f(t), g(t), h(t)\rangle =f(t)\textbf{i}+g(t)\textbf{j}+h(t)\textbf{k}. \end{equation*}
    For a given value of \(t_0\text{,}\) \(\textbf{r}\) is the position vector of a point \(P \left( f(t_0), g(t_0), h(t_0)\right)\) on a curve \(C.\)

    Definition 8.

    • If limits of the component functions exist,
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{t\rightarrow t_0} \textbf{r}(t)=\langle \lim_{t\rightarrow t_0} f(t), \lim_{t\rightarrow t_0} g(t),\lim_{t\rightarrow t_0} h(t)\rangle \end{equation*}

      ðŸ”—
    • For all \(t\) for which the limit exists,
      \begin{equation*} \textbf{r}'(t)=\lim_{t\rightarrow t_0}\frac{\textbf{r}(t+\Delta t)-\textbf{r}(t)}{\Delta t} \end{equation*}
      ðŸ”—

      \begin{equation*} \textbf{r}'(t)=\langle f'(t),g'(t),h'(t)\rangle \end{equation*}

    Definition 12.

    If \(\textbf{r}(t)=f(t)\textbf{i}+g(t)\textbf{j}+h(t)\textbf{k}\) is a smooth function, the length of the smooth curve traced by \(\textbf{r}\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} s=\int_a^b \sqrt{[f'(t)]^2+[g'(t)]^2+[h'(t)]^2}dt=\int_a^b \|\textbf{r}'(t)\|^2dt. \end{equation*}
    A curve in the plane or in space can be parameterized in terms of the arc length \(s\text{.}\)

    Definition 18.

    Let \(\textbf{r}(t)\) be a vector function defining a smooth curve \(C.\) If \(s\) is the arc length parameter and \(\textbf{T}=\frac{d\textbf{r}}{ds}\) is the unit tangent vector, then the curvature of \(C\) at point is
    \begin{equation*} \kappa=\left\|\frac{d\textbf{T}}{ds}\right\|. \end{equation*}
    The radius of curvature \(\rho\) is \(\frac{1}{\kappa}.\)
    ðŸ”—
    Furthermore, the velocity of the particle moving along the curve \(C\) is \(\textbf{v}(t)=\textbf{r}'(t)\text{,}\) whereas its speed is \(\frac{ds}{dt}=\nu=\|\textbf{v}(t)\|.\) The acceleration \(\textbf{a}(t)\) can be written in the following formats:
    • \(\displaystyle \textbf{a}(t)=\nu\frac{d\textbf{T}}{dt}+\frac{d\nu}{dt}\textbf{T}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(\textbf{a}(t)=\kappa\nu^2\textbf{N}+\frac{d\nu}{dt}\textbf{T},\) where \(\textbf{N}(t)=\frac{d\textbf{T}/dt}{\|d\textbf{T}/dt\|}. \) The scalar functions \(a_\textbf{T}=d\nu/dt\) and \(a_\textbf{N}=\kappa\nu^2\) are called tangential and normal components of the acceleration, respectively.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(\displaystyle \textbf{a}(t)=\frac{ds}{dt}\frac{d\textbf{T}}{dt}+\frac{d^2s}{dt^2}\textbf{T}.\)

    

    To solve a PDE on the domain whose shape is a 2D disk, it is much more convenient to represent the solution in terms of the polar coordinate system than in terms of the usual Cartesian coordinate system. For example, the behavior of the drum surface when hitting it by a stick would be best described by the solution of the wave equation in the polar coordinate system. We will discuss the following topics related to BVP in other coordinate systems (Chap 14):

    Remark 1. Relationship between Polar Coordinates and Rectangle Coordinates.

    \begin{align} x=r\cos\theta \amp \amp y=r\sin\theta \tag{1}\\ r^2=x^2+y^2 \amp \amp \tan\theta=\frac{y}{x} \tag{2} \end{align}
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 3. Laplace’s Equation in Polar Coordinates.

    The Laplace’s equation in \(R^2\) in terms of the Cartesian \((x,y)\) coordinate system is:
    \begin{equation} \nabla^2 u=\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}+\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}=u_{xx}+u_{yy}=0.\tag{3} \end{equation}
    From Remark 1, we can compute partial derivatives of \(r, \theta\) with respect to \(x, y\) from (2) using implicit differentiation:
    \begin{align} 2r\frac{\partial r}{\partial x}=2x, \amp \amp 2r\frac{\partial r}{\partial y}=2y \notag\\ \Rightarrow \frac{\partial r}{\partial x}=\frac{x}{r}=\cos\theta, \amp \amp \frac{\partial r}{\partial y}=\frac{y}{r}=\sin\theta \tag{4} \end{align}
    \begin{align} \sec^2\theta\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}=-\frac{y}{x^2},\amp \amp \sec^2\theta\frac{\partial\theta}{\partial y}=\frac{1}{x} \notag\\ \Rightarrow \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}=-\frac{y\cos^2\theta}{x^2}=-\frac{\sin\theta}{r}, \amp \amp \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial y}=\frac{\cos^2\theta}{x}=\frac{\cos\theta}{r} \tag{5} \end{align}
    Next, we start rewriting \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\) and \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\) using Chain Rule:
    \begin{align} \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \amp \frac{\partial u}{\partial r}\frac{\partial r}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial u}{\partial \theta}\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \notag\\ = \amp \cos\theta\frac{\partial u}{\partial r}-\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\frac{\partial u}{\partial \theta}\tag{6} \end{align}
    \begin{align} \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}= \amp \frac{\partial u}{\partial r}\frac{\partial r}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial u}{\partial \theta}\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial y} \notag\\ =\amp \sin\theta\frac{\partial u}{\partial r}+\frac{\cos\theta}{r}\frac{\partial u}{\partial \theta} \tag{7} \end{align}
    Then we further compute \(\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}\) and \(\frac{\partial ^2 u}{\partial y^2}\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} = \amp \frac{\partial }{\partial x}\left\{\cos\theta\frac{\partial u}{\partial r}-\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\frac{\partial u}{\partial \theta}\right\}\\ \frac{\partial }{\partial x}\left\{\cos\theta\frac{\partial u}{\partial r}\right\}=\amp \frac{\partial \cos\theta}{\partial x}\frac{\partial u}{\partial r}+\cos\theta\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left\{\frac{\partial u}{\partial r}\right\} \\ = \amp-\sin\theta\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}\frac{\partial u}{\partial r} +\cos\theta\left\{\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial\theta\partial r}\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial r^2}\frac{\partial r}{\partial x}\right\} \\ =\amp \frac{\sin^2\theta}{r}\frac{\partial u}{\partial r} +\cos\theta\left\{-\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial \theta\partial r}+\cos\theta\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial r^2}\right\} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x } \left\{\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\frac{\partial u}{\partial \theta}\right\} =\amp \frac{\partial \frac{\sin\theta}{r}}{\partial x}\frac{\partial u}{\partial \theta}+\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\frac{\partial }{\partial x}\left\{\frac{\partial u}{\partial\theta}\right\}\\ = \amp \frac{\partial u}{\partial \theta}\left\{\frac{\partial \frac{\sin\theta}{r}}{\partial \theta}\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial \frac{\sin\theta}{r}}{\partial r}\frac{\partial r}{\partial x}\right\} \\ \amp +\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\left\{\frac{\partial ^2 u}{\partial \theta^2}\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial \theta\partial r}\frac{\partial r}{\partial x}\right\} \\ =\amp\frac{\partial u}{\partial \theta}\left\{\frac{-2\cos\theta\sin\theta}{r^2}\right\}+\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial\theta^2}\left\{-\frac{\sin^2\theta}{r^2}\right\}\\ \amp +\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial\theta\partial r}\left\{\frac{\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r}\right\} \end{align*}
    Thus,
    \begin{align} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}=\amp \frac{\sin^2\theta}{r}\frac{\partial u}{\partial r} -\frac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r}\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial \theta\partial r}+\cos^2\theta\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial r^2}\notag\\ \amp +\frac{2\cos\theta\sin\theta}{r^2}\frac{\partial u}{\partial \theta}+\frac{\sin^2\theta}{r^2}\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial\theta^2}\tag{8} \end{align}
    Similarly, we can derive
    \begin{align} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2} =\amp \frac{\cos^2\theta}{r}\frac{\partial u}{\partial r} +\frac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r}\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial \theta\partial r}+\sin^2\theta\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial r^2}\notag\\ \amp -\frac{2\cos\theta\sin\theta}{r^2}\frac{\partial u}{\partial \theta}+\frac{\cos^2\theta}{r^2}\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial\theta^2}\tag{9} \end{align}
    Adding (8) and (9), we obtain
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}+\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}=\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial r^2}+\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial u}{\partial r}+\frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial\theta^2}. \end{equation*}
    Thus, the Laplace’s equation in polar coordinates is
    \begin{equation} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial r^2}+\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial u}{\partial r}+\frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial\theta^2}=0\tag{10} \end{equation}

    Definition 7. Cylindrical Coordinates.

    • Heat Equation in polar coordinate system:
      \begin{equation*} u_t=k\left(u_{rr}+\frac{1}{r}u_r+\frac{1}{r^2}u_{\theta\theta}\right), k>0:\mbox{diffusivity} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    • Wave Equation in polar coordinate system:
      \begin{equation*} u_{tt}=c^2\left(u_{rr}+\frac{1}{r}u_r+\frac{1}{r^2}u_{\theta\theta}\right), c>0:\mbox{wave velocity} \end{equation*}
      The above two equations are in cylindrical coordinates since the time \(t\) gives the 3rd axis from the circular plate which produces a cylinder with infinite length as \(t\gt 0.\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Laplacian in Cylindrical Coordinates: Consider a domain of a cylinder for the Laplace’s Equation: \(x=r\cos\theta,y=r\sin\theta, z=z\)
      \begin{equation*} \nabla^2 u=\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial r^2}+\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial u}{\partial r}+\frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial \theta^2}+\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial z^2} \end{equation*}

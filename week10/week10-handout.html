    Definition 1. 1D Heat Equation.

    \begin{equation} c\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}=\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}\tag{1} \end{equation}
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.

    Similar to what we did in Week 9, we need to replace the PDE with approximation of derivatives. For \(x,\) we still use central difference,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}\approx \frac{1}{h^2}\left[u(x+h,t)-2u(x,t)+u(x-h,t)\right] \end{equation*}
    For \(t,\) we use forward difference to avoid "negative time",
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}\approx\frac{1}{k}\left[u(x,t+k)-u(x,t)\right]. \end{equation*}
    Substitue them to (1), we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2}{h^2}\left[u(x+h,t)-2u(x,t)+u(x-h,t)\right]=\frac{1}{k}\left[u(x,t+k)-u(x,t)\right]. \end{equation*}
    Let \(\lambda=\frac{ck}{h^2}\text{,}\) the equation can be simplified to
    \begin{equation*} u_{i,j+1}=\lambda u_{i-1,j}+(1-2\lambda)u_{i,j}+\lambda u_{i+1,j} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.

    Remark 5. Stability.

    A numerical method is unstable if round-off errors or any other errors grow too rapidly as the computations proceed. It can be proved that the precedure is stable if \(\lambda\leq 0.5\text{,}\) but unstable otherwise. For a small \(k\) and small \(h\text{,}\) \(\lambda\) is unlikely satisfied the stability condition of less than or equation 0.5.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 6. Crank-Nicholson Method.

    The algorithm consists of replacing the 2nd order PDE in the heat equation by the average of two central differences, one evaluated at \(t\) and the other at \(t+k\text{:}\)
    \begin{align} \amp \frac{c}{2}\left[\frac{u(x+h,t)-2u(x,t)+u(x-h,t)}{h^2} +\frac{u(x+h,t+k)-2u(x,t+k)+u(x-h,t+k)}{h^2}\right] \tag{2}\\ = \amp \frac{1}{k}\left[u(x,t+k)-u(x,t)\right] \notag \end{align}
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 7.

    The Crank-Nicholson method is an implicit finite difference method. It requires that we solve a system of equations to determine the approximate value of \(u\) on \((j + 1)\)-st time line. However, implicit methods do not suffer from instability problems.
    🔗
    Again, we let \(\lambda=\frac{ck}{h^2}.\) After rearranging (2), we can write it as
    \begin{equation} -u_{i-1,j+1}+\alpha u_{i,j+1}-u_{i+1,j+1}=u_{i+1,j}-\beta u_{i,j}+u_{i-1,j},\tag{3} \end{equation}
    where \(\alpha=2\left(1+\frac{1}{\lambda}\right)\) and \(\beta=2\left(1-\frac{1}{\lambda}\right),\) \(j=0,1,\ldots,m-1,\) and \(i=1,2,\ldots,n-1.\) At \((j+1)\)-st timeline, we have \(n-1\) equations (one for each interior point), which looks like \(\textbf{A}\textbf{u}_{:,j+1}=\textbf{b}\text{,}\) where
    \begin{equation*} \textbf{A}=\left[\begin{array}{ccccc} \alpha \amp -1 \amp 0 \amp \cdots \amp 0\cr -1 \amp \alpha \amp -1\amp \amp\cr 0\amp -1\amp \alpha \amp \ddots\amp\cr \vdots\amp \ddots \amp \ddots \amp \ddots\amp\cr 0\amp 0\amp \cdots\amp -1\amp \alpha\end{array} \right], \textbf{b}=\left[\begin{array}{c} u_{2,j}-\beta u_{i,j}+u_{0,j}+u_{0,j+1}\cr u_{3,j}-\beta u_{2,j}+u_{1,j}\cr u_{4,j}-\beta u_{3,j}+u_{2,j}\cr \vdots\cr u_{n,j}-\beta u_{n-1,j}+u_{n-2,j}+u_{n,j+1}\end{array}\right] \end{equation*}
    Since \(\textbf{A}\) is a tridiagonal matrix. \(\textbf{A}\textbf{u}_{:,j+1}=\textbf{b}\) can be solved efficiently.
    🔗

    Definition 9. 1D Wave Equation.

    \begin{equation} c^2\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}=\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2}\tag{4} \end{equation}
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 10.

    Use 2 central differences, we replace (4) by
    \begin{align} \amp \frac{c^2}{h^2}\left[u(x+h, t)-2u(x,t)+u(x-h,t)\right] \tag{5}\\ =\amp \frac{1}{k^2}\left[u(x,t+k)-2u(x,t)+u(x,t-k)\right] \notag \end{align}
    Let \(\lambda=\frac{ck}{h} \text{,}\) then (5) yields
    \begin{equation} u_{i,j+1}=\lambda u_{i+1,j}+2(1-\lambda^2)u_{i,j}+\lambda^2 u_{i-1,j}-u_{i,j-1}, \tag{6} \end{equation}
    for \(i=1,2,\ldots,n-1,\) and \(j=1,2,\ldots,m-1.\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 12.

    Use Finite Difference to approximate the solution of the BVP:
    \begin{align} \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2}=\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2}\amp \amp 0\lt x\lt 2, 0\lt t\lt 1 \tag{7}\\ u(0,t)=1,u(2,t)=0, \amp \amp 0\leq t\leq 1 \tag{8}\\ u(x,0)=e^{-16(x-1)^2}, \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}\bigg|_{t=0}=0 \amp \amp 0\leq x \leq 2 \tag{9} \end{align}
    with \(n=5\text{,}\) and \(m=10\text{.}\)

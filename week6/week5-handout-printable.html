    Remark 1. Review of inner product between two vectors.

    The inner product of two \(n\times 1\) vectors \(u\) and \(v\) is defined as
    \begin{equation*} (u,v)=u^tv=\sum_{i=1}^n u_iv_i. \end{equation*}
    A key property is
    \begin{equation*} u^tv=0\iff u \mbox{ and }v \mbox{ are perpendicular to each other.} \end{equation*}
    It means if \(u^tv=0,\) then the projection of \(u\) onto \(v\) is 0 and the projection of \(v\) onto \(u\) is 0. This makes decomposition of vectors cleaner.

    Definition 3.

    Two function \(f_1\) and \(f_2\) are said to be orthogonal on an interval \([a,b]\) if
    \begin{equation*} (f_1,f_2)=\int_a^bf_1(x)f_2(x)dx=0. \end{equation*}
    A set of real-valued functions \(\{\phi_1(x),\phi_2(x),\phi_3(x),\ldots\}\) is said to be orthogonal on an interval \([a,b]\) if
    \begin{equation*} (\phi_i,\phi_j)=\int_a^b \phi_i(x)\phi_j(x)dx=0, \forall i\neq j. \end{equation*}

    Theorem 17. Conditions for Convergences.

    Let \(f\) and \(f'\) be piecewise continuous on the interval of \([-p,p];\) that is, let \(f\) and \(f'\) be continuous except at a finite number of points in the interval and have only finite discontinuities at these points. Then for all \(x\) in the interval \((-p,p)\) the Fourier series of \(f\) converges to \(f(x)\) at a point of continuity. At the point of discontinuity, the Fourier series converges to the average
    \begin{equation*} \frac{f(x+)+f(x-)}{2}, \end{equation*}
    where \(f(x+)\) and \(f(x-)\) denote the limit of \(f\) and \(x\) from the right and from the left, respectively.
